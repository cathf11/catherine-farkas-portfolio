Case of Study 2: Optimizing Help Desk Operations

Interactive Dashboard (Power BI)

Open in a new tab for a full view

*Note: You can interact with the filters and slicers directly on the embedded dashboard.

The Business Problem & the Approach

The objective was to solve a core operational challenge: a Help Desk manager has thousands of tickets but no clear view of team performance. They couldn't easily spot bottlenecks or manage agent workload effectively.

To solve this, I designed a dashboard with a strong focus on data segregation and user experience (UX). I split the tool into two logical views:

  • Tickets evolution: To track process KPIs (ticket types, priorities, ratings).
  • Agent Performance: To manage people (a sortable ranking to see top/bottom performers).

To improve usability, I also implemented two custom features: a Dark Mode toggle for accessibility and a collapsible Help Guide that explains every chart and button.

The result is a tool that empowers a manager to stop analyzing raw data. They can now see the 'big picture' (e.g., 'ticket ratings are dropping') and instantly click the 'Agent' tab to see who needs support.

Case of Study 3: COVID-19 in Europe

Geospatial & BI Analysis

*Note: This is the full data analysis presentation, hosted on Google Slides.

The Business Problem & the Approach

The objective was to visualize the worldwide COVID-19 pandemic, with a specific focus on transforming scattered data into a clear analysis of the impact on European countries.

To achieve this, I implemented a multi-tool workflow. The process began with geospatial analysis in QGIS to map the global spread of cases, deaths, and recoveries.

Following the necessary ETL (data cleaning and preparation), I moved the data into Power BI. There, I built bar charts to rank countries and scatter plots to analyze the statistical correlation between confirmed cases, fatalities, and recoveries.

CF VizTech: The Art of Choosing Charts (Part I)

By Catherine Farkas (CF VizTech)

As a Data Analyst, one of the ongoing challenges when building a Power BI dashboard is the "blank canvas." The goal isn't just to show data, but to make it tell a story.

To do this, mastering the visual language is vital: understanding the types of charts that exist and what each is used for. In this guide, we explore the main types of comparison charts.

1. Column Chart

Represents data in vertical columns, allowing for comparisons between different categories.

Each column represents a specific value, making it easy to quickly and clearly compare data based on height.

2. Bar Chart

A variation of the column chart that uses horizontal bars to represent data.

It is ideal for comparing categories with long names or numerous options.

The choice between columns and bars depends on how you want to present your data and the story you want to tell!

3. Grouped Column Chart

Perfect for comparing multiple data series. They show the value of different subcategories (e.g., months) within each category (e.g., products).

The comparison between groups and subgroups makes it easy to distinguish variations.

4. Stacked Column Chart

Shows how various parts contribute to the total of a set.

You can see how sales from different months add up within each product, all in a single column. Ideal for visualizing the total and its constituent parts.

5. 100% Stacked Bar Chart

Similar to the stacked column chart, but each bar always sums to 100%.

It is ideal for comparing the proportions of different subcategories, showing how the parts are distributed relative to the total, regardless of the total size.

6. Line Chart

Shows the evolution of data over time or across categories. It is ideal for visualizing trends and patterns.

Commonly used to analyze continuous changes.

7. Column and Line Chart

Combines absolute values and trends to compare data on different scales.

Allows highlighting trends while visualizing exact values, such as sales and percentage growth.

8. Radar Chart

Represents multiple variables in a polygonal format, where each axis corresponds to a different variable.

Allows for the visual comparison of several elements in relation to these variables (at least three are needed).

9. Tables

Structure information in rows and columns. They display detailed data, facilitating clear and orderly comparison and analysis.

Perfect for seeing all the details at a glance!

CF VizTech: The Art of Choosing Charts (Part II)

By Catherine Farkas (CF VizTech)

In Part I, we laid the foundation for comparison. Now, we continue our journey through the "art of choosing charts."

In this guide, we dive into visualizations that allow us to discover hidden Relationships, understand the Distribution of our data, analyze Segmentation, and explore Geographical patterns.

1. Scatter Plot (Relationship)

Graphically represents the relationship between two quantitative variables. For example, when analyzing Price and Sales.

If the points align upwards, it suggests that a higher price might lead to more sales. It's useful for identifying patterns and trends.

2. Bubble Chart (Relationship)

Represents three dimensions of data: two on the X and Y axes, and the third through the size of the bubbles.

Ideal for analyses involving multiple variables, highlighting patterns and relationships.

3. Word Cloud (Relationship)

A grouping of key terms related to a topic, where the most important or repeated words are highlighted in a larger size.

To improve readability, it's recommended to keep the words aligned and use colors that contrast well.

4. Pie Chart (Segmentation)

Useful for seeing how parts of a whole are distributed, such as percentages or proportions.

Be careful! If you have many categories or small differences, it can be hard to read. An alternative is the donut chart.

5. Treemap (Segmentation)

Uses rectangles to represent categories, where the size of each shows its relative importance.

Colors add more details or differences. It is ideal for visualizing how data is distributed at a single glance.

6. Funnel Chart (Segmentation)

Shows how data narrows through the stages of a process. It starts with a large quantity and ends with a smaller one.

Ideal for areas like marketing and sales, allowing visualization of the customer journey.

7. Box Plot (Distribution)

Visualizes the distribution of a dataset, showing the minimum, maximum, median, and quartiles.

It is ideal for identifying dispersion, detecting outliers, and comparing distributions.

8. Histogram (Distribution)

Represents the frequency distribution of a dataset by grouping values into intervals or ranges.

It allows identifying patterns such as skewness and concentration in different areas.

9. Map (Geographical)

Shows the geographical distribution of data. In this case, a bubble map, where the size of each bubble indicates a value.

It facilitates the comparison of data in different locations and helps identify patterns.

CF VizTech: 5 Steps for Data Storytelling

By Catherine Farkas (CF VizTech)

Data is more than just numbers! Data Storytelling is the art of turning data into visual narratives that inform and inspire decisions, making information more accessible.

Join me as we unravel a real sales case and learn to tell stories with data.

  1. 1. Know Your Audience

    Before you start, understand who you're addressing. Who will see this sales analysis? The management team? Marketing? You must tailor your story to what they need to know and act upon.

  2. 2. Establish the Context

    The chart shows sales by region during Q1. Your objective: Investigate the sales drop in Region A. This places your audience in the story from the beginning.

  3. 3. Introduce the Characters

    The key characters in this story are: Sales (protagonist), Region A (where the problem is), and the Marketing team (who has influence). These elements represent the forces at play.

  4. 4. The Conflict

    Here comes the conflict: Sales in Region A have fallen by 59% during the last two months of Q1. Why? The conflict is what holds interest and guides the investigation.

  5. 5. The Resolution

    Resolution: By digging into the data, you discover the decrease is due to a reduction in the Marketing budget.

    Recommendation: Increase Marketing investment to recover sales. You've told a story that leads to an actionable solution.

    This final step is the most critical. It's where the analyst transforms data "chaos" (the conflict) into "clarity" that provides value. We don't just present a problem; we deliver a clear, data-backed roadmap that the leadership team can execute.

Let's Recap

Data Storytelling allows you to connect data with strategic decisions, moving from isolated charts to a narrative with:

  • Audience
  • Context
  • Characters
  • Conflict
  • Resolution

CF VizTech: The 3 Types of Analysis in BI

By Catherine Farkas (CF VizTech)

From Figures to Strategy

Business Intelligence (BI) is the key to transforming large volumes of data into valuable information that drives strategic decisions.

Each type of analysis (descriptive, predictive, and prescriptive) provides a unique insight. Discover how each one helps you make smarter decisions!

  1. 1. Descriptive Analysis: What happened?

    Descriptive analysis tells you what has happened in the past. It's like a summary that helps you understand historical behavior and trends. Perfect for seeing how things have unfolded. It's the foundation on which we build the next analyses.

  2. 2. Predictive Analysis: What could happen?

    Predictive analysis uses historical data to make predictions about future events. It uses statistical techniques and algorithms to project possible outcomes and trends. It's like having an advance view of what might happen. Ideal for making proactive decisions.

  3. 3. Prescriptive Analysis: What should we do?

    It goes beyond predicting; it offers recommendations on the best actions to take to achieve desired results. For example, if data suggests that improving customer service will increase satisfaction, it will recommend implementing certain actions. It's the guide to making effective strategic decisions!

In Summary

  • 1. Descriptive: Summary (past)
  • 2. Predictive: Projection (future)
  • 3. Prescriptive: Recommendation + action

CF VizTech: How to Transform a Chart in 4 Steps

By Catherine Farkas (CF VizTech)

A good chart doesn't just show data; it tells a clear story. Often, the default chart is full of "noise." Let's transform this cluttered chart into one that is clean and insight-focused.

The "Before": A Cluttered Chart

Loud colors, background, borders, labels everywhere... It's hard to know what to look at.

  1. 1. White Background

    We remove the background color and border. Always use white for the background and a soft gray for secondary elements.

  2. 2. Uniform Bar Color

    We apply a uniform gray color to all bars. We use color (blue) only to strategically highlight what's important: Product C (most sales) and Product A (the one we want to boost).

  3. 3. Simplify Labels and Axes

    We remove unnecessary axis titles (it's already understood). We remove decimals and add the ‚Ç¨ symbol to the Y-axis. We simplify the bar labels, highlighting only those for products A and C.

  4. 4. Title and Gridlines

    We soften the gridlines to a very light gray so they don't compete. Finally, we change the generic title ("Sales by categories") to an *actionable insight*: "Product A recovers from last month's drop."

Result: The "After"

This chart is clean, easy to read, and directs the user's attention exactly to the message we want to convey.

Remember the 4 Steps:

  • 1. White background.
  • 2. Uniform bar color (and highlight with color).
  • 3. Simplify labels and axes.
  • 4. Title with an insight (not generic).
